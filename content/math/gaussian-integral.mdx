## The Integral

The Gaussian integral is one of the most beautiful results in all of mathematics:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx = \sqrt{\pi}
$$

Despite the simplicity of the statement, no elementary antiderivative of $e^{-x^2}$ exists. The proof requires a clever dimensional trick.

## Proof via Polar Coordinates

Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx$. Then:

$$
I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2} \, dx\right)\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2} \, dy\right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-(x^2 + y^2)} \, dx \, dy
$$

Switching to polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ where $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$:

$$
I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2} \, r \, dr \, d\theta = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi
$$

Therefore $I = \sqrt{\pi}$. $\blacksquare$

## Generalization

The general Gaussian integral with parameter $a > 0$:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ax^2} \, dx = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}
$$

This is the foundation of the **normal distribution** in probability:

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

## Numerical Verification

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import integrate

# Numerical integration of the Gaussian
result, error = integrate.quad(lambda x: np.exp(-x**2), -np.inf, np.inf)

print(f"Numerical:  {result:.10f}")
print(f"Analytical: {np.sqrt(np.pi):.10f}")
print(f"Error:      {abs(result - np.sqrt(np.pi)):.2e}")
```

The result confirms $I = \sqrt{\pi} \approx 1.7724538509$.
